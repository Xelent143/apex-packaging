# Poly bag thickness — when 1.5 mil is enough and when you need 4.

Polybag thickness ("mil") is the most common spec procurement teams get wrong — usually over-specifying, which doubles the bag cost for no real protection benefit. Here's how to size it correctly.

## What "mil" actually measures

One mil = one one-thousandth of an inch (0.001"). A 1.0 mil bag is half as thick as a 2.0 mil bag. The thickness affects:

- **Puncture resistance** — higher mil resists sharp edges and corners.
- **Tear strength** — higher mil resists tear propagation.
- **Stretch** — lower mil stretches more under load (sometimes desirable, often not).
- **Cost** — material cost scales roughly linearly with mil.

It does **not** strongly affect: oxygen barrier, moisture barrier, light blocking (those depend on resin and coatings, not thickness).

## Thickness by use case

### 0.5 – 1.0 mil — light retail and produce
Wicketed produce bags, light garment bags, retail point-of-sale bags. Use only for soft, light items with no sharp edges.

### 1.0 – 1.5 mil — light shipping
General light retail and e-commerce. Apparel, soft goods, light accessories. Most low-cost polymailers run 1.2–1.5 mil exterior, often with a 1.0 mil cushion lining.

### 1.5 – 2.0 mil — standard shipping
Most general-purpose shipping bags for products without sharp edges. Auto parts (smooth), small electronics in inner packaging, dry goods.

### 2.0 – 3.0 mil — moderate-weight shipping
Heavier products, items with mild edges, moderate stacking. Common for hardware, fasteners, food packaging (under board carton).

### 3.0 – 4.0 mil — heavy goods and industrial
Heavy parts, palletized small items, mattress overwrap, products with sharp corners (small metal hardware). The default for industrial subassembly bagging.

### 4.0 – 6.0 mil — premium protection and large items
Furniture overwrap, mattress bags, large industrial parts, applications with stacking pressure or rough handling.

## When higher isn't better

Common over-spec scenarios where 4 mil is bought but 2 mil would have worked:

- Smooth-edged products under 5 lb being palletized — 2 mil is fine.
- Apparel and soft goods — 1.5 mil is usually plenty.
- Small electronics in their own box — the bag just needs to be a dust barrier, 1.5–2 mil is fine.

When higher truly is necessary:

- Anything with metal edges, threads, or corners — step to 3 mil minimum.
- Mattress and furniture — 4 mil or above is the standard.
- Long-distance freight without inner packaging — 3 mil minimum to survive cross-dock handling.

## Material matters as much as thickness

A 2 mil LLDPE bag is roughly as strong as a 3 mil LDPE bag in puncture resistance. The right specification looks at both material and gauge:

- **LDPE** — flexible, low cost, lower strength per mil.
- **LLDPE** — better puncture and tear at lower mil. Default for industrial.
- **HDPE** — stiffer, higher strength, lower clarity. Used for produce and dispensers.
- **MDPE** — middle ground.
- **Anti-static (pink LDPE)** — for electronics; thickness as needed for handling.

If you're choosing between higher LDPE mil and lower LLDPE mil, LLDPE almost always wins on cost per unit of strength.

## A practical specification approach

1. **Product weight** — under 5 lb (light), 5–25 lb (medium), 25+ lb (heavy).
2. **Edge sharpness** — smooth (no upgrade), moderate (1 mil up), sharp (2 mil up).
3. **Handling environment** — gentle (no upgrade), parcel/LTL (1 mil up), bulk truck or hand-tossed (2 mil up).
4. **Sum the upgrades** against your starting mil for the weight class.

Example: a 12 lb metal fitting going LTL. Start at 2 mil (medium weight). +2 for sharp edges. +1 for LTL handling. Spec at 5 mil — or step to LLDPE at 3 mil to save cost.
