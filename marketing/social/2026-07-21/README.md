## LinkedIn Post

Food packaging quotes usually get expensive after approval, not before it.

That happens when the buyer sends dimensions but leaves out the route, the board target, the liner expectation, or the real pack count.

For dry goods cartons and retail multipacks, a stronger brief usually answers:

1. What product is going into the carton, and what are the exact dimensions and weight?
2. Is the package built for shelf display, case packing, pallet shipment, ecommerce fulfillment, or a mix of those routes?
3. Does the board need stronger stacking performance, grease or moisture resistance, or a cleaner retail finish?
4. Is artwork ready, or does the team still need dieline and print-prep support?
5. Does the program need samples to confirm fit, closure behavior, and packing speed before production?

That matters because a lower first quote does not help if the board changes, the pack count shifts, or the pallet plan breaks after testing.

The latest Apex guide walks through how buyers can define food packaging boxes around route, board choice, liners, samples, and reorder planning before comparing price.
